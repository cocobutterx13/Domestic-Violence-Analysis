Overview
Why It Matters
Domestic violence disproportionately affects women across all demographics. This project investigates whether socioeconomic factors (education, employment, income, and marital status) can predict the likelihood of experiencing domestic violence.
Key Research Questions
01 How do socioeconomic factors (education, employment, income) relate to domestic violence vulnerability?
02 Does marital status influence exposure to or experience of domestic violence?
03 Which demographic and contextual factors are most predictive of vulnerability when modelled jointly?

Income Analysis
Income: A Complex Picture
Income is heavily zero-inflated (>50% report zero). Log-transformation (log1p) was applied for modelling.

Conclusions
Key Takeaways
01 Employment & marital status are the strongest and most consistent predictors of domestic violence risk in this sample.
02 Higher income (log-transformed) is significantly associated with reduced odds of violence, suggesting financial independence as a protective factor.
03 Some multicollinearity exists (notably income vs employment), requiring cautious interpretation.
04 Model is reasonably well-calibrated despite class imbalance affecting sensitivity.
05 Socioeconomic interventions (employment support, income security, education access) may represent meaningful pathways to reducing domestic violence risk.
